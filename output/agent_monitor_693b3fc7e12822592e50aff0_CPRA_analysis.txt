### 3. Specific Violations of CPRA
Even without the sample log, the **configuration** of this agent introduces definitive, non-speculative compliance violations:

*   **Violation of Data Minimization (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100(c)):**
    *   **The Issue:** The agent is configured to indiscriminately harvest the full `inputs` and `outputs` from the "Recruiting" agent and dump them into a generic Google Sheet.
    *   **The Risk:** Monitoring agents should typically track *performance* (success/fail, latency). By capturing the full content payload (e.g., candidate resumes, interview notes, salary negotiations), the agent is collecting more personal information than is "reasonably necessary" for the stated purpose of system monitoring.

*   **Violation of Security Procedures (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100(e)):**
    *   **The Issue:** Sensitive HR and Board-level PII/SPI is being moved from a presumably secure agent environment into an unstructured, externalized Google Sheet (`1G0n...`).
    *   **The Risk:** Google Sheets often lack the granular audit logging, access controls, and encryption-at-rest standards required for HR systems of record. If the sheet is shared broadly within the "mftestco.com" domain, it constitutes an internal data breach (Unauthorized Access).

*   **Inability to Honor Consumer Rights (DSARs) (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.105):**
    *   **The Issue:** By creating a "Shadow IT" database (the Google Sheet), you sever the link between the data and the central system of record (e.g., the ATS or HRIS).
    *   **The Risk:** If a job applicant exercises their "Right to Delete," your organization will delete their record from the main database but will likely fail to delete the row in this specific Google Sheet. This residual data retention is a direct CPRA violation.
